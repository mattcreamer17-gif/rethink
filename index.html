The frame

60/30/10: which layer each piece of the stack belongs on.

Most AI work breaks down into three layers once you look at it honestly. Teams who skip this distinction spend budget putting LLMs where Postgres would do the job faster and with better guarantees. The orchestration question is which parts of each product belong on which layer, and which pieces can be shared across all four product lines instead of rebuilt four times.

60%

Traditional code and databases

Scheduling, storage, session-note routing, audit trails, compliance flags, roll-ups.

Example here: most of the AI Dashboard's alerting logic is rules plus good data plumbing.

30%

Rule-based logic

Deterministic policy, eligibility checks, threshold triggers, workflow state machines.

Example here: payor-side fraud-and-abuse screens and dosage-guidance guardrails.

10%

Genuine AI

The part that actually benefits from a model, judged against the cost of running it.

Example here: Session Note AI on top of shared plumbing, and the cohort-finding inside RethinkFutures.

The methodology

Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM).

The audit runs on a published methodology. ICM treats context as a layered filesystem with explicit interpretability properties, which lines up with how your AI Code of Ethics page talks about transparency and human oversight. One methodology behind the marketing, not a black box.

Read the paper on GitHub (RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-). Submitted to ACM TiiS, MIT license.

Questions we walk in with

Five things worth ten minutes on the call.

  1. When RethinkBH ships a new AI feature, how many people inside the other three product lines can use the same pipeline the next morning?
  2. The 530-million-point clinical dataset is the moat. Who owns the decision on which slices of that dataset feed which product?
  3. If the new CEO asks you in week six to pick one AI investment that pays back in nine months, is that answer already on the desk, or does it need a cross-product working group?
  4. Where do you draw the line between an AI feature and an orchestration layer for the three buyers who don't share procurement: schools, practices, employers?
